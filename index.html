Integrating Writing and Design Portfolio

Design Beginnings


Design Fuego

The goal of this project was to establish a digital identity using HTML, CSS, and GitHub Pages, demonstrating an understanding of web structure, styling, and online publishing. The Design Fuego project showcases my ability to code and structure a personalized web space. By modifying index.html and style.css, I applied principles of web hierarchy, typography, and visual balance to create a cohesive digital portfolio. The site reflects a strategic use of contrast, spacing, and readability, ensuring usability and engagement.

Logo A/B/C

The goal of this project was to create three distinct logos using Canva and Adobe Express, incorporating color theory, typography, contrast, and repetition to establish a personal or professional brand identity. The Miles Howell logo set presents a playful yet structured approach to branding. The bold color palette (orange and purple) establishes strong brand recognition, while font weight, iconography, and composition variations explore different visual identities. The designs balance symmetry and asymmetry, demonstrating adaptability across different branding contexts.

UX Testing Logo Project

The goal of this project was to conduct a user experience study, gather feedback on logo designs, and apply Don Norman's Three Levels of Processing to refine branding choices. This study implemented UX research methods, including visceral (first impressions), behavioral (usability), and reflective (long-term impact) analysis. Participants' insights guided potential logo revisions, ensuring the final design aligned with user expectations. The study demonstrates my ability to bridge design intuition with data-driven refinement, reinforcing the iterative nature of UX design.

Cartoon Portrait

The goal of this project was to translate a photographic image into a vector-based digital illustration using Adobe Illustrator and core graphic design tools like the Pen Tool, Layers, and Color Fill. The self-portrait illustration showcases expressive line work and color mapping, emphasizing stylized contouring while maintaining recognizable features. Integrating a realistic cat with a digital self-portrait highlights the contrast in texture and artistic interpretation, demonstrating versatility in mixed-media digital composition.

Good Design

The goal of this project was to analyze an example of high-quality editorial design, applying principles like contrast, negative space, typography, and composition to evaluate its effectiveness. The Vogue cover analysis dissects how type interacts with imagery, using layering, alignment, and spacing to create depth. The breakdown emphasizes color contrast, particularly the interplay between dark skin tones and crisp white fabric, reinforcing how design directs visual hierarchy. This project showcases technical writing, modular text placement, and graphic annotation, reinforcing an ability to analyze critically and present design choices.

Course Goals


1. Consider the role of rhetoric, technology, and ethics in acts of making.

Throughout this course, my work has explored the interplay between visual rhetoric, technological tools, and ethical considerations in design. The Vogue cover analysis, for example, demonstrates how typography, contrast, and composition shape meaning, reinforcing the rhetorical power of editorial design. My Miles Howell logo collection further reflects these principles, as branding decisions influence public perception and engagement. Digital tools like Adobe Express, Illustrator, InDesign, and Canva highlight the evolving role of technology in modern design, while ethical considerations—such as accessibility, representation, and audience impact—remain at the forefront of my creative decisions.

2. Leverage Design Principles.

Each project was a deliberate application of contrast, balance, alignment, and hierarchy. The "Good Design?" poster dissects how professional layouts utilize negative space, color theory, and type hierarchy to create compelling visuals. Similarly, my logo suite demonstrates repetition, proximity, and contrast, ensuring brand cohesion across multiple formats. These projects display an evolving mastery of intentional design choices, proving that strong design relies on a foundation of strategic visual organization.

3. Engage with others in a workshop environment.

Collaboration and critique have been integral to my creative process. Engaging with peers allowed me to refine my work based on feedback loops, testing different iterations before finalizing designs. The UX study and its subsequent presentation exemplified user-centered design thinking, where audience responses dictated any refinements. This iterative approach mirrors real-world design practices, proving that design is most effective when shaped by dialogue and adaptability.



4. Practice revision within and across media forms.

Revising my work across different platforms and media types has strengthened my ability to adapt designs based on context. The evolution of my design portfolio—from static images to interactive elements in my Design Fuego website—illustrates a growing fluency in cross-platform storytelling. The juxtaposition of illustration, digital branding, and editorial analysis demonstrates an ability to transition between print, web, and interactive design, reinforcing the value of revision in crafting polished, versatile compositions.

5. Learn to present your work.

Presenting my design motivations has been just as crucial as creating the work itself. Whether through the UX study presentation, design breakdowns, or this digital portfolio, I've honed my ability to articulate design decisions with clarity and intent. The Google Slides deck and Design Fuego site function as curated showcases of my work, balancing visual engagement with structured storytelling. This course has stressed that a designer's role is not just to create—it's to communicate the why behind the design, ensuring that aesthetic choices align with strategic goals.
